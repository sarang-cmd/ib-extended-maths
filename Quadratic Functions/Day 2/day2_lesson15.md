# Lesson 15: Building a Quadratic from Clues

> Note: This guide gives exercise **references only** from the H&H books, not full copied questions.
> Use the book for the original exercise text.

## Focus
Find equations from roots, vertex, intercepts, and graph features.

## Concepts list
- constructing functions
- factor form
- vertex form
- using a point
- parameter a

## Teach me
1. If you know the roots, start with y=a(x-p)(x-q). If you know the vertex, start with y=a(x-h)^2+k.
2. The unknown a controls stretch and direction, so use one extra point to determine it.
3. This kind of question tests whether you understand structure, not just procedures.
4. Always start from the form that matches the information given. That reduces algebra and reduces mistakes.
5. Once the function is found, expand only if the question asks for standard form.

## What to be able to do
- Explain the idea in your own words.
- Move between equation and graph.
- Notice which form of a quadratic is most useful.
- Check whether your answer is mathematically and contextually sensible.

## Study moves
- Spend 10 minutes reviewing the examples before touching exercises.
- Do 3-5 easier questions first, then 2-3 harder ones.
- Write one sentence after each question: “What idea did this question really test?”
- Keep an error log with two columns: mistake and fix.

## IB-style conceptual questions
1. Why does knowing two roots not determine a unique quadratic by itself?
2. A graph touches the x-axis at x=3 and passes through (0,18). Explain why a repeated factor must appear.
3. Which information is more powerful: two roots and one point, or vertex and one point? Discuss.
