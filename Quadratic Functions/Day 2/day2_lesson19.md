# Lesson 19: Modelling and Word Problems

> Note: This guide gives exercise **references only** from the H&H books, not full copied questions.
> Use the book for the original exercise text.

## Focus
Translate context into quadratic equations or functions.

## Concepts list
- modelling
- variable choice
- realistic constraints
- checking reasonableness

## Teach me
1. Word problems become manageable once you define the variable clearly. Bad variable choice makes easy algebra feel hard.
2. Many rectangle, area, motion, and profit problems naturally produce quadratic equations.
3. After solving, check whether both roots make sense in context. Mathematics can generate formal solutions that reality rejects.
4. Units matter. A correct number with the wrong interpretation is not a complete solution.
5. This is where conceptual understanding shows up most clearly.

## What to be able to do
- Explain the idea in your own words.
- Move between equation and graph.
- Notice which form of a quadratic is most useful.
- Check whether your answer is mathematically and contextually sensible.

## Study moves
- Spend 10 minutes reviewing the examples before touching exercises.
- Do 3-5 easier questions first, then 2-3 harder ones.
- Write one sentence after each question: “What idea did this question really test?”
- Keep an error log with two columns: mistake and fix.

## IB-style conceptual questions
1. Why must contextual solutions be filtered, while pure algebra problems may keep all real solutions?
2. A quadratic model gives two positive times for a projectile. Explain what each time could mean physically.
3. What is the mathematical advantage of writing a word problem as a function rather than only as an equation?

## Exit check
- Can you explain this lesson without using the textbook wording?
- Can you identify the key feature of a quadratic in less than 20 seconds?
- Can you tell which method or representation is most efficient here?
