# Lesson 9: Sketching from Minimal Information

> Note: This guide gives exercise **references only** from the H&H books, not full copied questions.
> Use the book for the original exercise text.

## Focus
Sketch accurately from intercepts, vertex, and symmetry.

## Concepts list
- efficient sketching
- minimum data
- graph features
- consistency checks

## Teach me
1. You rarely need a full table of values to sketch a quadratic well. A strong sketch usually needs opening, vertex, axis of symmetry, and intercepts if they exist.
2. A good check is internal consistency. If the axis says x=2, then points at x=1 and x=3 should sit at the same height.
3. Sketching from minimal information is valuable because exam questions often expect reasoning, not calculator dependence.
4. When data is incomplete, use what is fixed and leave proportions approximate. Accuracy of structure matters more than artistic scale.
5. This is where all previous lessons start working together.

## What to be able to do
- Explain the idea in your own words.
- Move between equation and graph.
- Notice which form of a quadratic is most useful.
- Check whether your answer is mathematically and contextually sensible.

## Study moves
- Spend 10 minutes reviewing the examples before touching exercises.
- Do 3-5 easier questions first, then 2-3 harder ones.
- Write one sentence after each question: “What idea did this question really test?”
- Keep an error log with two columns: mistake and fix.

## IB-style conceptual questions
1. What is the minimum information needed to distinguish between two quadratics that share the same roots?
2. Why can a sketch be mathematically valid even when exact scale is not perfect?
3. A student plots three points correctly but draws a non-symmetric curve. Explain why the graph must still be wrong.
