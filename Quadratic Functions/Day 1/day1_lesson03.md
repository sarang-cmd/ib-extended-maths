## Focus
Move from numerical patterns to graph behaviour.

## Concepts list
- tables of values
- symmetry
- curvature
- concave up
- concave down

## Teach me
1. A table of values can reveal a quadratic pattern. If first differences are not constant but second differences are constant, that is a major clue you are looking at a quadratic relationship.
2. Quadratic graphs are symmetric. This means points equally far from the axis of symmetry have the same y-value.
3. That symmetry is not cosmetic. It is the reason the turning point sits exactly halfway between equal-height points on the graph.
4. If a is positive, the graph has a lowest point. If a is negative, the graph has a highest point.
5. When sketching, aim for structure, not artistic perfection: opening, turning point, symmetry line, and intercepts matter most.

## What to be able to do

## IB-style conceptual questions
1. A table has constant second differences. Is that alone enough to guarantee the relation is a quadratic function? Explain carefully.
2. A parabola passes through (1,5) and (5,5). What can you infer immediately without knowing the full equation?
3. Why is symmetry a stronger idea than just saying the graph “looks balanced”?
