# Lesson 2: Structure Through Expansion and Factorisation

> Note: This guide gives exercise **references only** from the H&H books, not full copied questions.
> Use the book for the original exercise text.

## Focus
See how quadratic expressions are built and decomposed.

## Concepts list
- expansion
- factorisation
- binomial product
- roots in factor form
- equivalent forms

## Teach me
1. Quadratics often come from multiplying two linear expressions. For example, (x-p)(x-q) expands to x^2-(p+q)x+pq.
2. That pattern matters because the numbers in factor form carry direct graph information: p and q become the x-intercepts when the function equals zero.
3. Factor form is especially powerful because it shows where the graph meets the x-axis. Standard form is better for seeing the y-intercept, and vertex form is better for the turning point.
4. Good students switch forms depending on the question. Great students know why each form is useful.
5. When a quadratic does not factor nicely over the integers, that does not mean it is not quadratic. It only means factorisation is not the simplest tool.

## What to be able to do

## IB-style conceptual questions
1. Why does factor form tell you more directly about x-intercepts than standard form does?
2. Two different quadratics have the same x-intercepts. Must they be the same graph? Explain completely.
3. Construct a quadratic with roots 2 and -5 that opens downward, then justify why infinitely many such functions exist.
